Pink Floyd · 1994 · A listening guide

The Division Bell

About this album

Pink Floyd's first album in nine years, recorded without Roger Waters following his acrimonious departure in 1985. David Gilmour and Richard Wright led the sessions, held partly on Gilmour's houseboat studio moored on the Thames. Lyricist Polly Samson — Gilmour's partner — shaped much of the album's emotional content, which circles around the theme of communication and the failure to connect. It was the band's last studio album before a 20-year silence.

1

Cluster One

5:58

instrumentalambientopeningspace

The story

The album drifts open like waking from a dreamless sleep — no drums, no guitars, just a slow wash of synthesiser. It is an invitation to stillness before the album's central subject announces itself: the distance between people, and the struggle to bridge it.

The music

Richard Wright's keyboards create a vast, unhurried ambient landscape — patient in the way that only Floyd, at their best, could be patient. The track earns its five minutes simply by existing in the space it creates, with no urgency to get anywhere.

Something to know

The album was recorded partly on David Gilmour's houseboat studio, The Astoria, moored on the Thames. That floating, on-the-water quality is audible in this opening — nothing is anchored, everything drifts slightly. It is one of the most genuinely peaceful openings in their catalogue.

2

What Do You Want from Me

4:20

relationshipsfrustrationdisconnectionGilmour

The story

The album's most visceral rocker arrives like a door slammed open. It is about the exhaustion of a relationship where you have given everything and still cannot satisfy the other person — the bewildered, slightly angry question of someone who genuinely does not know what more they can offer.

The music

Gilmour's guitar here is direct and slightly ragged — less patient than usual, more insistent. After the ambient drift of Cluster One, the directness is deliberate and almost shocking. The song does not wander. It states, repeats, and demands an answer.

Something to know

Most of the album's lyrics were written by Polly Samson — Gilmour's partner and later wife. She drew on their early relationship and the difficulties of loving someone as emotionally guarded as Gilmour. Many of the album's most personal moments are her words delivered in his voice.

3

Poles Apart

7:04

Syd BarrettRoger Waterslossnostalgia

The story

The most emotionally loaded song on the album. It contains two verses: one addressed to Syd Barrett, one to Roger Waters — the two men who defined Pink Floyd and then, in different ways, left it. Gilmour mourns both losses in the same breath, across the same vast distance.

The music

The song builds from a quiet, searching guitar figure into something vast and aching. Gilmour's slide guitar in the extended outro is among the most expressive playing of his career — less like a solo than like someone trying to say something that words have failed to convey.

Something to know

The line addressed to Syd — did you know it was you all along — suggests Gilmour always understood who the real genius was. And the verse about Waters shows the wound was still raw years after the split. This song asks whether brilliance and friendship can survive the distance people put between themselves.

4

Marooned

5:29

instrumentalisolationGilmour guitarGrammy winner

The story

A purely instrumental piece and perhaps the most purely beautiful thing on the album. The title says everything: someone stranded, cut off, surrounded by vast emptiness. No lyrics are needed. The guitar expresses a loneliness that words would only diminish.

The music

Gilmour's slide guitar moves with the patience of someone who has accepted their situation. There is no panic, no urgency — just a slow, aching beauty. It is often cited as one of his finest performances on record. The sustained notes hang in the air like something left unsaid.

Something to know

The track won the Grammy Award for Best Rock Instrumental Performance in 1995 — one of the very few Grammy wins in Pink Floyd's career. It beat competition from Joe Satriani and the Stevie Ray Vaughan estate, which gives some sense of just how extraordinary the playing is.

5

A Great Day for Freedom

4:17

Berlin Walldisillusionmenthistoryhope

The story

One of the most overlooked songs in the Floyd catalogue — written about the fall of the Berlin Wall, not as a celebration but as a meditation on what followed. The wall came down, and the world discovered that freedom is not a simple thing. The problems did not dissolve; they just took different shapes.

The music

Gilmour's vocal is unusually tender — almost fragile. The song does not soar or triumph. It walks quietly, as if through the aftermath of something enormous that has left everyone slightly stunned. The melody carries both hope and its deflation in the same breath.

Something to know

The song was written while the euphoria of November 1989 was still in the air, but recorded as the complications began to emerge. Gilmour was watching the news with the same attention Floyd had always brought to political events — and finding, as usual, that the truth was more complicated than the headline.

6

Wearing the Inside Out

6:49

Richard Wrightdepressionisolationthe self

The story

Richard Wright's only lead vocal on the album — and his most confessional moment since the early 1970s. The song is about depression and emotional isolation: the experience of turning entirely inward, away from others, wearing the inside out so that your inner turmoil becomes your external face.

The music

Wright's voice has an extraordinary quality here — soft, slightly uncertain, as if he is not entirely sure he should be saying these things out loud. The restraint is what makes it devastating. Anthony Moore's lyrics cut directly to the experience of depression without melodrama.

Something to know

Wright had been largely sidelined during the Roger Waters era of the band. The Division Bell was partly a restoration of his place in Floyd. This song — his to sing, his to own — was a significant moment of recovery, both musical and personal. He had been away, turned inward. Now he was back.

7

Take It Back

6:13

regretloveadmissionsingle

The story

A song about recognising, too late, that you were wrong — in how you treated someone, in what you chose. The title has a double meaning: taking back what was said and done, and taking back the relationship. The admission is sincere; whether it is heard is another matter entirely.

The music

Released as a single, it is the album's most commercially shaped track — wider, more radio-friendly than the surrounding songs. The production has a slight sheen that sets it slightly apart, though Gilmour's guitar in the outro finds its way back to the album's more introspective territory.

Something to know

The song was used in a Volkswagen advertisement in the mid-1990s — a fairly unusual step for Pink Floyd, who had historically resisted commercial use of their music. It gave the album a wider reach than it might otherwise have had, and remains the track most casual listeners recognise from it.

8

Coming Back to Life

6:19

renewalawakeninglovenumbness ending

The story

The emotional turning point of the album. After songs about distance, isolation and walls, this one is about emergence — waking up after a long period of numbness, the world coming back into focus. It is one of the most quietly joyful things Floyd ever recorded, and all the more affecting for how unexpected the feeling is.

The music

The guitar intro is one of Gilmour's most tender — clean, unhurried, each note given room to breathe. The song does not rush to its subject. It arrives the way the feeling it describes arrives: gradually, almost cautiously, as if afraid of being wrong about the light returning.

Something to know

Polly Samson has said the song is partly about her relationship with Gilmour — finding someone who brought her back to herself. The Division Bell understands that recovery and despair coexist, often in the same room. This is its most hopeful window, placed deliberately at the album's centre of gravity.

9

Keep Talking

6:11

communicationStephen Hawkingconnectionlanguage

The story

The album's thesis statement, built around a speech by Stephen Hawking: "For millions of years, mankind lived just like the animals. Then something happened which unleashed the power of our imagination. We learned to talk." The song is about what we risk when we stop.

The music

Hawking's synthesised voice is woven into the fabric of the track — not as a gimmick but as the song's beating heart. The groove underneath is one of the most driven things Floyd ever recorded. Nick Mason's drums push it forward with real urgency, a reminder that the band could still move.

Something to know

Hawking agreed to appear after hearing the concept — he was an admirer of Pink Floyd. Gilmour saw Hawking's life as a demonstration of the very thing the song argues for: that as long as communication exists, something essential survives. They met several times during the recording. Both men were clearly moved by the collaboration.

10

Lost for Words

5:15

Roger Watersbitternessestrangementolive branch

The story

Almost certainly addressed to Roger Waters — an olive branch that was never accepted. The lyrics describe a friendship destroyed by bitterness, an attempt at reconciliation met with contempt, and the exhausted resignation that follows. It is one of the saddest songs on the album precisely because its gesture goes unanswered.

The music

Gilmour's vocal is weary — the voice of someone who has tried and been refused. The arrangement is gentle, almost resigned, which gives the lyrics room to land without drama. The song does not shout. It simply states, and waits, and when no reply comes, it continues without one.

Something to know

Waters has never publicly acknowledged the song. His departure from Pink Floyd in 1985 was acrimonious. They briefly reunited for the Live 8 concert in 2005, sharing a stage for the first time in 24 years. That night — not this song — was the closest thing to a resolution the public ever witnessed.

11

High Hopes

8:32

childhoodnostalgiatimeCambridge

The story

One of the most magnificent closing tracks in Pink Floyd's entire catalogue. About childhood, the passage of time, and the distance between who you were and who you became. The bells that open it are real Cambridge bells — recorded in Gilmour's hometown. Everything in the song is about looking back across an unbridgeable distance.

The music

The arrangement builds slowly and with great dignity, from the opening bells through an aching guitar figure to a climax of genuine grandeur. Gilmour's vocal is measured and unhurried — the voice of someone who has made peace with loss, or at least learned to carry it. B.J. Cole's pedal steel adds a yearning that suits the song perfectly.

Something to know

This was the last song on the last Pink Floyd album before a 20-year gap. As a goodbye, it is almost unbearably fitting. The final image — of the endless river flowing, and of high hopes fading as the world turns — became the title and concept of their 2014 instrumental album. High Hopes did not close a chapter. It turned out to be the final word.
