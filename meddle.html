Pink Floyd · 1971 · A listening guide

Meddle

Tap each track as it begins to play

About this album

The album that changed everything for Pink Floyd. After a period of uncertainty following Syd Barrett's departure, Meddle was the band finding their own voice without him. Recorded at several London studios through 1971, it moves from the aggressive opener to the pastoral, the playful, and finally to Echoes — a 23-minute piece that anticipated everything they would go on to achieve. This is where modern Pink Floyd truly begins.

1

One of These Days

5:57

aggressionbass-drivenmenaceopening

The story

The album crashes open with one of the most viscerally exciting things Pink Floyd ever recorded — two bass guitars in stereo, panned left and right, running at each other like a storm front. There are almost no lyrics, just a single spoken line delivered through a vocoder: "One of these days I'm going to cut you into little pieces." The voice belongs to Nick Mason — his only lead vocal on any Pink Floyd record.

The music

The dual bass opening — Waters on one side, Gilmour on the other — creates an unnerving stereo effect that rewards headphones enormously. Listen for the way the two lines interlock and occasionally clash. The track builds through a series of accelerating sections to a release that feels genuinely cathartic.

Something to know

The spoken line is a reference to a BBC radio comedy called The Goon Show. The Goons were a major influence on early Pink Floyd — the band's name itself is partly derived from two Goon Show characters, Pink Anderson and Floyd Council. That single threatening line is the only moment of aggression on the whole album — everything else is patient and expansive.

2

A Pillow of Winds

5:11

acousticpastoralsleeptenderness

The story

After the violence of the opening, the album settles into something almost unbearably gentle. A soft acoustic ballad about the peace of sleeping beside someone you love — the pillow of winds is the feeling of complete safety, warm and still, with the world outside held at bay. It is one of the most quietly beautiful things Pink Floyd ever recorded.

The music

Gilmour's voice here has a hushed, half-awake quality that perfectly suits the subject. The acoustic guitar barely disturbs the air. Richard Wright's organ drifts in like first light through a curtain — present but not insistent. The whole arrangement feels like it is trying not to wake anyone up.

Something to know

The contrast between this track and the one before it is deliberate and total. Floyd understood that albums were not collections of similar songs — they were journeys with varying emotional weather. Going from "One of These Days" to "A Pillow of Winds" is like stepping out of a thunderstorm into a warm room. Both require each other to work fully.

3

Fearless

6:08

defiancefolkself-beliefLiverpool

The story

A song about climbing — literally and figuratively. About doing the thing that frightens you, regardless of what others say. The imagery is of a man ascending a hill while the crowd below mocks him. He goes anyway. By the end he's at the top, and the crowd has gone home. There is something genuinely moving in its quiet stubbornness.

The music

The song fades out with the sound of Liverpool Football Club's Anfield crowd singing "You'll Never Walk Alone" — a recording of actual supporters captured at a match. It is one of the most unexpected endings in the Floyd catalogue: a folk rock song dissolving into a football terrace in full voice, thousands of people singing about not being alone. It works perfectly.

Something to know

Gilmour has described the song as being about someone who simply decides not to be afraid — which sounds obvious, but the music makes it feel like a genuine act of will. The crowd at Anfield singing at the end is not ironic. Pink Floyd, at this point in their career, were sincere in ways that would become harder to sustain as they got bigger and more famous.

4

San Tropez

3:43

playfuljazzholidayRoger Waters

The story

The album's most unexpected track — a breezy, almost jazzy little number about lounging in the South of France. Written by Roger Waters, it has none of his usual darkness. It is simply charming: a man enjoying himself by the sea, waiting for his lover to arrive, everything warm and unhurried. It is proof that Waters could be light when he chose to be.

The music

The piano from Richard Wright is genuinely playful — the whole band sounds relaxed in a way they rarely allowed themselves. The arrangement has a lounge-jazz feel that sits oddly but pleasantly on a Pink Floyd album. It is the sound of a band taking a day off inside their own record.

Something to know

Waters has said he wrote the song partly as a joke — a deliberate exercise in lightness to prove the point that he could. It appeared on the album without much fanfare and has always been a slightly surprising moment for new listeners. Coming after "Fearless" and before "Seamus", it works as a kind of deep breath — a reminder that the album is not trying to be relentlessly heavy.

5

Seamus

2:13

humourbluesdogSteve Marriott

The story

The album's most gloriously ridiculous track — a blues number where a dog named Seamus howls along to the music. The dog belonged to Steve Marriott of Humble Pie, who was staying nearby. They recorded a track, played it back, the dog responded, and they captured it. It is genuinely funny and not trying to be anything else.

The music

A loose, warm blues improvisation with Gilmour on slide guitar and Seamus providing the vocal. The dog's contributions are perfectly timed in a way that suggests either remarkable instinct or extraordinary luck. Whatever the explanation, the result is charming. Slide guitar and dog: two instruments in conversation.

Something to know

Seamus was later renamed "Mademoiselle Nobs" in live performances, and Roger Waters' dog Nobs took over the howling duties on stage during the 1971 tour. Waters has said that of all the strange things Floyd did in those years, bringing a dog onstage to howl the blues was perhaps the strangest — and his personal favourite.

6

Echoes

23:31

epichumanityocean23 minutes

The story

One of the greatest pieces of music Pink Floyd ever made — and one of the great extended compositions in rock. At 23 minutes, it occupies the entire second side of the original vinyl. It begins with a single sonar ping from Richard Wright's piano and ends somewhere vast, cosmic, and deeply human. The subject is connection: the improbable fact that consciousness exists at all, and that it recognises itself in other people across the distance of the world.

The music

The track moves through at least six distinct sections: the gentle opening, a full band passage of extraordinary beauty, a disturbing middle section of shrieking feedback and abstract sound — like a nightmare inserted into a dream — and then a long, patient resolution back to the opening theme. The final minutes are among the most transcendent in the band's catalogue. Listen with headphones if you can.

Something to know

The piece was assembled from over 30 short improvisations the band called "Nothing Parts 1–24." They developed it over months, slowly discovering the shape it wanted to be. Stanley Kubrick famously considered using it in 2001: A Space Odyssey as an alternative to Also Sprach Zarathustra — and when you hear it, that almost-history makes complete sense. It is music about the size of existence.
